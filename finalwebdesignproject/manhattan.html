Manhattan

More on the Borough

The borough of Manhattan was Lenape land that eventually evolved into one of the most famous and storied metropolises in the world, ushering in early waves of colonization, immigration, and commerce that molded its future. Manhattan’s central geographic placement on the Atlantic coast made it invaluable as a massive harbor and hub for trade, helping to secure the borough’s relationship to economic power (https://www.britannica.com/place/Manhattan-New-York-City).

From row houses to Art Deco to Gilded Age mansions to iconic skyscrapers, Manhattan architecture reflects vast commerce, complexity, and historical roots. Architectural artifacts of New York’s colonial period include chapels and taverns, which eventually gave way to Greek Revival and Beaux-Arts. Later, with further financial and population growth, commercial architecture exploded in the Midtown and Financial Districts, while Harlem churned out poetry, music, and art that contributed greatly to Manhattan’s cultural landscape and worldwide influence. Now known as one of the most expensive places to live in America, neighborhoods across Manhattan contain both preserved history and modern elements that reflect a diverse urban footprint. Despite rampant gentrification, longstanding communities still persist, their history is still visible (https://architecture-history.org/schools/NEW%20YORK,%20USA.html).

Manhattan is the home to one of the most famous skylines in the world, including massive structures such as the Freedom Tower (built in the wake of the 9/11 attacks), Empire State Building, Flatiron Building, and Chrysler Building, along with the core transportation centers of New York Penn Station and Grand Central Terminal.

Chrysler Building

The Chrysler Building is a famous Art Deco skyscraper built in 1930 and designed by William Van Alen. Its height and glamor makes it a fixture of Manhattan architecture (https://skyscraper.org/tallest-towers/chrysler-building/).

Empire State Building

The Empire State Building is a symbolic powerhouse of New York City, completed in 1931 and designed by the architectural firm Shreve, Lamb & Harmon in the Art Deco style. It was the world’s tallest building up until 1970, when the World Trade Center was built (https://www.architecturaldigest.com/story/the-empire-state-building-everything-you-need-to-know).

Grand Central Station

Grand Central Terminal is a train station built in the Beaux-Arts architectural style by firms Reed & Stem, and Warren & Wetmore. Grand Central is known for its ceiling constellation mural and marble staircases, which make it a beloved New York City attraction in addition to a huge commuter rail (https://blog.mcny.org).
